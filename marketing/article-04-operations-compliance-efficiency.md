# The FQHC Operations Playbook: How to Serve More Patients With the Staff You Already Have

*A mid-size California FQHC can generate 30-50 billable encounters per provider per day through team-based care — without hiring a single new physician. Here's the operational model, the billing rules, and the compliance guardrails.*

---

At a $225 PPS rate, a provider who sees 18 patients per day generates $4,050 in encounter revenue. A provider embedded in a team-based care model — rotating between exam rooms while 3-5 RNs handle the bulk of each visit — generates 30-50 encounters per day, or $6,750-$11,250 in daily revenue from the same provider salary.

The difference is $540,000-$1,440,000 in annual revenue per provider, depending on scale and payer mix.

This is not a theoretical model. It is the operational standard at high-performing FQHCs nationwide, and the math is particularly favorable in California where PPS rates tend to be higher than the national average. Yet most California FQHCs still operate in a traditional model where each provider sees 16-22 patients per day in sequential appointments.

The gap between current operations and optimized operations at most FQHCs is not a staffing problem. It is a workflow problem, a billing knowledge problem, and a compliance confidence problem. This article addresses all three.

## The Team-Based Care Economics

The foundational concept: separate what requires a licensed provider from what does not.

In a traditional FQHC visit, a medical assistant rooms the patient, a provider conducts the entire visit (history, exam, assessment, plan, documentation), and the MA handles checkout. The provider's time is the bottleneck, and every minute spent on tasks that an RN, LVN, or MA could perform is a minute not spent seeing additional patients.

In a team-based model, the workflow inverts:

**Medical Assistant responsibilities (pre-visit):** Rooming, vitals, medication reconciliation, point-of-care labs (glucose, A1C, rapid strep, urinalysis), immunization administration, care gap identification from EHR alerts. Under California Business and Professions Code sections governing medical assistants, MAs can perform all of these tasks independently under standing protocols — no direct provider supervision required for each instance.

**RN responsibilities (visit bulk):** Chronic disease management assessment, medication titration under protocol, patient education, care coordination, PHQ-9/GAD-7 screening, wound care, phone triage, and pre-visit planning. RNs operating under physician-approved protocols can manage 80% of a routine chronic disease visit without the provider present.

**Provider responsibilities (face-to-face):** Assessment, diagnosis, treatment plan modification, procedure performance, and documentation sufficient to support a billable encounter. The critical compliance requirement: the provider must perform and document a face-to-face encounter. Co-signing an RN's note without seeing the patient does not create a billable visit and constitutes fraud.

**The rotation model:** One provider works across 3-5 exam rooms, each staffed by an RN. While the provider performs a face-to-face assessment in Room 1, RNs in Rooms 2-5 are conducting the bulk of their respective visits. The provider rotates through rooms on a 5-8 minute cycle. Each room produces a complete billable encounter.

**Breakeven math:** At a $225 PPS rate and approximately $1,400/day fully-loaded provider cost (salary, benefits, malpractice), breakeven is 6-7 encounters per day. Everything above that is margin. A provider in a rotation model generating 35 encounters per day produces $7,875 in revenue against $1,400 in direct provider cost — a contribution margin of $6,475 per day, or $1.6 million annually per provider (before RN and MA costs).

The RN cost is real but manageable. Three RNs at $55/hour ($1,320/day for three) supporting one provider still leaves a daily contribution margin of $5,155 on 35 encounters. The model scales.

## The Billing Rules That Most FQHCs Get Wrong

Three billing rules shape FQHC operational strategy more than almost any clinical decision. Getting them right is the difference between leaving $200,000+ on the table annually and capturing it.

### Rule 1: Same-Day Billing Under Medi-Cal vs. Medicare

**Medi-Cal (WIC §14132.100):** When a patient sees both a medical provider and a behavioral health provider on the same day, Medi-Cal pays one PPS encounter. Not two. This is California law, not a billing error. The only planned same-day combination that generates two PPS encounters under Medi-Cal is medical plus dental.

**Medicare:** Pays two PPS encounters for same-day medical plus behavioral health (using modifier 59 on the second claim, typically G0467 + G0470). For FQHCs with 15-25% Medicare populations, this represents significant uncaptured revenue if BH visits aren't being documented and billed as separate encounters.

**Practical impact:** If your FQHC performs 50 same-day BH warm handoffs per month for Medicare patients and currently bills zero of them separately, that is approximately $135,000 in annual revenue sitting in your EHR unbilled. The fix is documentation training and modifier configuration — not hiring.

**The APM workaround:** California's FQHC Alternative Payment Methodology (launched July 2024) moves participating FQHCs from per-visit PPS to per-member-per-month capitation. Under APM, the same-day billing restriction is effectively moot because payment is not tied to individual encounters. FQHCs evaluating APM should model whether the capitation rate adequately compensates for the encounter volume their team-based model generates.

### Rule 2: Who Can Bill

Under PPS, only these provider types generate billable encounters: MD/DO, NP, PA, CNM, clinical psychologist, LCSW, and visiting RN (for specific home health services only).

RNs, LVNs, MAs, CHWs, and health educators do not independently generate PPS billable encounters.

**The January 2026 exception:** New Medi-Cal billing codes activated in January 2026 allow CHW encounters to be billed directly for the first time. This is the most significant change to CHW financial sustainability in a decade. FQHCs that have not yet trained their CHW workforce on documentation requirements and configured their EHR billing codes for CHW encounters are leaving money on the table every day the codes remain unused.

**Revenue estimate for CHW billing:** An FQHC with 10 CHWs averaging 6 billable encounters per day at a conservative $75 reimbursement rate generates $1.1 million annually in new revenue from a workforce that was previously a pure cost center.

### Rule 3: Scope-of-Practice Revenue Optimization

California's scope-of-practice laws define what each provider type can do independently, under supervision, or not at all. Most FQHCs operate well below top-of-license for every role — meaning they pay RN salaries for tasks that LVNs can perform, and provider salaries for tasks that RNs can perform.

FQHC Talent Exchange tracks the delegation matrix for 10 California FQHC roles across 15 clinical tasks, with Business and Professions Code citations for each delegation rule. The revenue-relevant highlights:

**NPs and PAs:** Full prescriptive authority and PPS billing capability. In California, NPs have had full practice authority since January 2023 (AB 890). An NP practicing at top-of-scope can manage a full primary care panel independently, generating the same PPS revenue as an MD at 60-70% of the compensation cost. FQHCs that still require physician co-signatures on NP encounters are adding administrative cost without clinical or billing benefit.

**Dental hygienists:** Under BPC §1907-1910, registered dental hygienists in alternative practice (RDHAP) can provide preventive dental services without a dentist present. An FQHC that deploys dental hygienists at full scope in satellite locations or mobile units can generate 400+ dental PPS encounters per month without proportional dentist staffing.

**RN chronic care management:** RNs managing chronic disease panels under protocol can bill CCM codes (99490) for Medicare patients — 20+ minutes of care management per month generates a separate payment outside of PPS. An FQHC with 100+ Medicare patients with 2+ chronic conditions should have a CCM billing program. Most do not.

## Compliance Guardrails: What Breaks and How to Prevent It

Every operational efficiency strategy creates compliance surface area. The three most common compliance failures in FQHCs pursuing operational optimization:

### Failure 1: Incident-To Billing Without Adequate Supervision

When RNs or MAs provide services "incident to" a provider's treatment plan, the provider must be present in the office suite (not just in the building) and the service must be part of a documented treatment plan the provider initiated. FQHCs that expand team-based care without tightening their incident-to documentation will eventually face audit findings.

**Prevention:** Document the treatment plan at the initial provider visit. Ensure the supervising provider is physically present in the same suite during RN/MA services. Audit 10 charts per month for incident-to compliance.

### Failure 2: Sliding Fee Scale Violations During Revenue Optimization

HRSA requires all FQHCs to offer a sliding fee scale based on federal poverty level. When an FQHC launches DPC subscriptions, employer contracts, or other non-traditional revenue streams, the sliding fee scale must remain available to all patients regardless of ability to pay. A DPC subscription can be offered as an option — never as a requirement or replacement for the sliding fee schedule.

**Prevention:** Maintain the sliding fee scale as the default. Offer DPC/subscription as an additive option. Document patient choice. Train front desk staff on the distinction.

### Failure 3: 340B Audit Failures

The 340B program is the single highest-margin revenue opportunity for most FQHCs, but HRSA's Office of Pharmacy Affairs conducts audits and the penalties for non-compliance include program exclusion. The most common failure: dispensing 340B-priced medications to patients who do not have a documented encounter at the FQHC establishing them as a patient of the covered entity.

**Prevention:** 100% audit trail in the 340B database linking every dispensed prescription to a qualifying patient encounter. Monthly self-audits of 340B eligibility screening. Annual external 340B audit before HRSA gets there first.

### Failure 4: AI Documentation Sign-Off Without Review

As FQHCs deploy ambient AI scribes, a new compliance risk is emerging: providers signing off on AI-generated documentation without reviewing it for accuracy. AI scribes can introduce hallucinated clinical details, incorrect medication dosages, or culturally inappropriate language. Under federal and state law, the signing provider is responsible for everything in the note.

**Prevention:** Require providers to actively edit (not just sign) AI-generated notes before closing them. Track the edit rate — a provider who signs 100% of AI notes without edits is not reviewing them. Establish a monthly AI documentation QA process where clinical leadership reviews a sample of AI-generated notes for accuracy.

## The SB 525 Compliance Clock

California's SB 525 requires all healthcare facilities — including FQHCs specifically — to pay a minimum wage of $25/hour by 2027. The direct cost is significant, but the hidden cost is larger: wage compression.

When the wage floor rises to $25/hour, every employee currently earning $25-$35/hour expects a proportional increase. Experienced MAs making $28/hour will not accept the same pay as new hires. Lead CHWs at $30/hour will expect $35+. Supervisors and coordinators at $35/hour will expect $40+.

The total cost of SB 525 compliance is typically 2-3x the direct minimum wage increase when compression is factored in.

**OKR to track this:**

Objective: Achieve full SB 525 wage compliance with offset revenue identified.

Key Results:
- Complete wage gap analysis across all positions within 30 days
- Present phased compliance plan to board with compression modeling by Month 2
- Identify $200K+ in revenue offsets (340B, ECM, RCM improvements, CHW billing) by Month 3
- Maintain 15%+ supervisor wage differential throughout implementation
- Zero wage-related grievances or turnover spikes during transition

## The Implementation Sequence

For an FQHC executive reading this and wondering where to start, the recommended sequence is driven by speed-to-revenue and implementation complexity:

**Month 1-2: Quick wins (low complexity, immediate revenue)**
Configure CHW billing codes in your EHR and train CHW staff on documentation. Begin billing Medicare co-visits with modifier 59. Baseline your current same-day BH visit volume. These require no capital investment and no new hires.

**Month 2-4: Workflow redesign (moderate complexity, high revenue)**
Pilot team-based care with one provider and 2-3 RNs in a rotation model. Measure encounter volume, revenue, and patient satisfaction against baseline. If successful, expand to additional providers. Launch RN-led CCM billing for Medicare patients with 2+ chronic conditions.

**Month 3-6: Revenue diversification (higher complexity, transformative revenue)**
Begin 340B entity-owned pharmacy planning. Scale ECM enrollment using EHR-based patient identification. Model DPC subscription for non-Medi-Cal patient population. Each of these requires capital investment and 3-6 months to reach steady-state revenue.

**Month 6-12: Structural transformation**
Evaluate APM vs. PPS based on your encounter mix. Explore IPA subsidiary if you have 50+ providers. Implement AI-powered revenue cycle management. Deploy ambient AI documentation with compliance guardrails.

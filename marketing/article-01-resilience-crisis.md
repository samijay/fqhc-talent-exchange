# California's FQHC Resilience Crisis: What the Data Actually Shows

*FQHC Talent Exchange scored all 214 California FQHCs across five dimensions of organizational resilience. The results should concern every board member in the state.*

---

On a Tuesday in February 2026, Indian Health Center of Santa Clara Valley filed a WARN Act notice and closed permanently. Twenty-one employees. The only tribal-serving FQHC in the county. Gone.

That same month, San Ysidro Health — one of the largest FQHCs in the nation, serving 107,000 patients in San Diego — laid off 75 community health workers and care coordinators. St. John's Community Health in Los Angeles cut 150 of its 1,200 employees. Alameda Health System announced 270 FTE reductions, the largest single FQHC layoff event FQHC Talent Exchange has tracked.

These are not isolated incidents. They are data points in a pattern.

FQHC Talent Exchange has tracked 20 confirmed workforce reduction events across California since early 2025, affecting 3,477 healthcare workers. Nine of those 20 events occurred at FQHCs specifically. The rest hit county health systems, health plans, and safety-net hospitals that contract with FQHCs — meaning the downstream impact on community health centers is even larger than the direct numbers suggest.

The question every FQHC board should be asking is not "will this happen to us?" but "how prepared are we when it does?"

To answer that, we built a resilience scorecard.

---

## How We Score Resilience

FQHC Talent Exchange evaluated all 214 California FQHCs across five dimensions, each weighted by its contribution to organizational survival during a funding crisis:

**Program Diversity (25% of score)** — How many revenue-generating programs does the FQHC operate? FQHCs that depend solely on Medi-Cal primary care visits face catastrophic risk when reimbursement changes. Those with ECM, CCM, behavioral health integration, dental, pharmacy, WIC, Ryan White, and PACE programs have multiple revenue streams that buffer against any single policy shock. This dimension carries the highest weight because program diversity is the single best predictor of financial resilience in the safety net.

**Quality Indicators (20%)** — HRSA quality scores, Glassdoor employee ratings, compliance history, and NHSC approval status. Quality matters for resilience because high-performing FQHCs attract better talent, retain staff longer, and qualify for bonus funding streams that lower-performing peers cannot access.

**Financial Positioning (20%)** — Funding vulnerability level, percentage of coverage population at risk from Medi-Cal changes, patient volume relative to site count, and revenue diversification markers. An FQHC serving 50,000 patients across 15 sites has a fundamentally different risk profile than one serving 5,000 patients from a single clinic.

**Workforce Stability (20%)** — Union status, layoff history, labor disputes, and workforce density (staff per site). FQHCs with union representation tend to have more transparent compensation structures and slower, more deliberate workforce reduction processes — buying time during crises. FQHCs with recent layoffs score lower because institutional knowledge loss compounds over time.

**Data Maturity (15%)** — EHR system modernity, directory completeness, and digital infrastructure. This dimension gets the lowest weight, but it matters more than most executives realize. An FQHC running a modern EHR (OCHIN Epic, eClinicalWorks, NextGen) can pivot to telehealth, deploy AI documentation tools, and generate quality reports that unlock bonus payments. An FQHC with no identifiable EHR system cannot.

Each FQHC receives a composite score from 0 to 100, mapped to letter grades: A (80+), B (65-79), C (50-64), D (35-49), F (below 35).

---

## What the Grades Reveal

The distribution is sobering.

The vast majority of California's 214 FQHCs cluster in the C and D range. Very few earn an A. This is not because California's FQHCs are poorly managed — many are extraordinary organizations doing heroic work. It is because the system they operate in was never designed to produce resilience. PPS reimbursement is flat. Grant funding is cyclical. Workforce pipelines are thin. And now, H.R. 1 has introduced the largest Medicaid funding reduction in U.S. history at the precise moment when FQHCs are being asked to absorb patients displaced from closing county clinics.

The FQHCs that score highest tend to share three characteristics:

First, they run five or more distinct programs. AltaMed Health Services — the highest-profile FQHC in the state — operates PACE, ECM, behavioral health, dental, pharmacy, WIC, and a dozen other programs across 50 sites. Its program diversity score is among the highest in the state. When one funding stream contracts, others expand. This is not accidental; it is the result of decades of strategic investment.

Second, they have modern EHR infrastructure. Of California's 214 FQHCs, 43 run OCHIN Epic, 34 run NextGen, 26 run eClinicalWorks, and 11 run standalone Epic. The remaining 92 either run legacy systems or have no identifiable EHR in our data. The correlation between EHR modernity and overall resilience is strong — not because the EHR itself confers resilience, but because the organizational capacity required to implement and maintain a modern EHR is the same capacity required to navigate a funding crisis.

Third, they have workforce stability. FQHCs with union representation (SEIU-UHW, NUHW, or SEIU local chapters) score higher on workforce stability not because unions prevent layoffs — they clearly don't, as Alameda Health System's 270-person reduction demonstrates — but because unionized FQHCs tend to have documented compensation structures, grievance processes, and transition protocols that preserve institutional knowledge even during reductions.

## The Regional Picture

The crisis is not evenly distributed.

Los Angeles County has the largest concentration of FQHCs and the most active hiring (258 open positions at AltaMed alone, representing 43% of all FQHC job postings statewide). But LA also has the most layoff events — St. John's (150 workers), LA County DHS (500 community health workers), and multiple smaller reductions across the county.

The Bay Area faces a different crisis: consolidation. Alameda Health System's 270 layoffs are the headline, but the deeper story is the $91.7 million working group the AHS board created in March 2026 to defer the cuts — a sign that even large systems are buying time rather than solving the structural problem. Meanwhile, Santa Clara County is cutting 365 FTE positions from its safety-net system, and LA County closed 7 clinics serving 15,000 patients in January.

San Diego's situation is acute. County supervisors voted 4-1 in March to overhaul the safety net, driven by $300 million in projected federal funding losses. San Ysidro Health opened a $55 million center the same month it laid off 75 workers — a paradox that only makes sense when you understand that capital funding and operating funding come from different streams with different timelines.

The Inland Empire and Central Valley are perhaps the most vulnerable regions. High Medi-Cal dependency (77% of San Joaquin Valley FQHC visits are Medi-Cal), fewer program diversification options, and thinner labor markets mean that layoffs in these regions create workforce gaps that take years to fill.

## What This Means for FQHC Leaders

The resilience scorecard is not a ranking designed to shame low-scoring organizations. It is a diagnostic tool. If your FQHC scores a D on program diversity, that tells you where to invest in the next 12 months. If you score an F on data maturity, that tells you that your inability to deploy AI documentation tools or generate quality reports will cost you revenue that higher-scoring peers are capturing.

Three actions every FQHC board should take before July 2026:

**Assess your PPS exposure.** California is eliminating PPS reimbursement for UIS (Undocumented Immigrant Services) patients effective July 1, 2026 — a policy change that represents up to $1 billion in sector-wide revenue impact. FQHCs with 30%+ undocumented patient volume face the most acute pressure. If your board has not modeled this scenario, you are 112 days from a revenue cliff you haven't measured.

**Audit your program portfolio.** Count your distinct revenue-generating programs. If the number is fewer than five, you are structurally fragile regardless of your current financial position. ECM enrollment, behavioral health integration, and dental expansion are the three fastest paths to program diversification for FQHCs that currently lack them.

**Benchmark your workforce.** Compare your staffing ratios, compensation structures, and turnover rates against peers in your region. FQHC Talent Exchange publishes salary benchmarks across 30 roles and 9 California regions — not to encourage poaching, but because FQHCs that don't know their market position can't compete for the talent that keeps them operational.

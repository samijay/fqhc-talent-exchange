# The AI Scribe Gold Rush: Why FQHCs Should Think Twice Before Signing

*Ambient AI documentation is the hottest product in healthcare. But the data tells a more complicated story for Federally Qualified Health Centers — and the vendors aren't telling you all of it.*

---

In February 2026, athenahealth made ambient AI documentation free for all customers. Not discounted. Free. Included in standard EHR updates.

One month earlier, Epic announced native AI charting capabilities baked into its platform. NextGen had already shipped Ambient Assist to rural FQHCs in Kentucky and Appalachia. eClinicalWorks partnered with Sunoh.ai and bundled it through NACHC Select pricing for community health centers nationwide.

If you run an FQHC in California and haven't been pitched an AI scribe in the last 90 days, your inbox is broken.

FQHC Talent Exchange tracks 22 AI adoption events across the FQHC sector — covering 8 vendors, 6 implementation categories, and a growing body of evidence about what happens after deployment. The picture that emerges is not the simple "burnout solution" that vendor slide decks present. It is more interesting, more nuanced, and for FQHCs specifically, more complicated than most executives realize.

---

## The Case for AI Scribes Is Real

Let's start with what the data actually supports.

**Clinician burnout is being reduced.** Abridge — which won Best in KLAS 2026 for ambient AI — reports that physician burnout rates dropped from 51.9% to 38.8% at Riverside Health System after deployment. Elation Health's integration with Anthropic's Claude shows 13 minutes saved per patient visit and 61% reduction in chart review time. NextGen reports that providers using Ambient Assist are eliminating "pajama time" — the after-hours documentation that drives physicians out of primary care.

**Scale is happening.** Sun River Health, the largest FQHC on the East Coast, has deployed Sunoh.ai across 500+ providers processing 7,000 visits per month. That is not a pilot. That is operational scale. Neighborhood Healthcare in California (30 facilities, 500,000+ annual visits) deployed Nabla with high clinician and patient satisfaction. Imperial Beach Community Clinic in San Diego reports 8-10 hours per week of documentation time savings per provider.

**Cost barriers are falling.** The NACHC-eClinicalWorks partnership offers bundled pricing through NACHC Select, making ambient AI accessible to member FQHCs at reduced cost. athenahealth made it free entirely. When the three EHR vendors that serve the majority of FQHCs (eClinicalWorks, NextGen, athenahealth) all include AI documentation at no additional cost, the question shifts from "can we afford it?" to "can we afford not to deploy it?"

The burnout crisis in FQHC primary care is not theoretical. California FQHCs are competing for physicians, nurse practitioners, and PAs against hospital systems and private practices that pay more and demand less documentation. Anything that reduces administrative burden is a retention tool — and in a market where FQHC Talent Exchange tracks 1,000+ open positions across the state, retention is the game.

## The Part the Vendors Don't Lead With

Here is where FQHC executives need to slow down.

A policy brief published in PMC in early 2026 analyzed what happens to coding patterns after ambient AI scribe deployment. The findings deserve more attention than they have received.

At one health system, deploying an AI scribe increased physician wRVUs (work relative value units) by 11% and HCC (hierarchical condition category) diagnoses per encounter by 14%. A separate JAMA study found that AI scribes added an average of 1.81 RVUs per physician per week — translating to approximately $3,044 in additional annual revenue per provider.

Is this appropriate documentation capture? Or is this coding inflation?

The answer matters enormously, but it matters differently for FQHCs than for fee-for-service hospitals.

**Under PPS reimbursement, most FQHCs cannot directly benefit from more intensive coding.** The Prospective Payment System pays a flat per-visit rate regardless of diagnosis complexity. If your AI scribe captures three additional HCC codes per visit, your PPS payment does not change. The scribe is not generating revenue — it is generating more thorough documentation, which is clinically valuable but financially neutral under PPS.

**But FQHCs in value-based contracts face a different calculus.** If your FQHC participates in Medicare Advantage capitation, Medi-Cal managed care risk arrangements, or ACO models, HCC coding directly affects your risk-adjusted payments. An AI scribe that systematically increases HCC capture could inflate your organization's risk profile — generating higher payments in the short term but triggering audit exposure, CMS scrutiny, and potential fraud liability in the long term.

This is not hypothetical. The PMC brief explicitly warns about a "coding arms race" in which health systems deploy AI scribes to maximize revenue capture rather than improve clinical documentation quality. The brief's authors — including researchers affiliated with major health systems — describe a scenario where competitive pressure forces every organization to deploy AI scribes defensively, not because the documentation is better but because failing to capture the same codes as peers means leaving money on the table.

For FQHCs, the defensive deployment trap is especially dangerous because the financial upside under PPS is minimal while the compliance risk is real.

## The EHR Platform Question

FQHC Talent Exchange tracks EHR adoption across all 214 California FQHCs. The distribution shapes which AI tools are available to which organizations:

Of California's 214 FQHCs, 43 run OCHIN Epic, 34 run NextGen, 26 run eClinicalWorks, 11 run standalone Epic, 5 run Cerner, and 3 run athenahealth. The remaining 92 either run legacy systems or have no identifiable EHR in our data.

This distribution creates a three-tier market:

**Tier 1: Free or bundled AI (100+ FQHCs).** If you run eClinicalWorks, NextGen, or athenahealth, your EHR vendor is either already shipping ambient AI or has it in preview. Cost: zero to minimal. Integration: native. Decision: when to turn it on, not whether to buy it.

**Tier 2: Platform-adjacent AI (54 FQHCs on Epic/OCHIN Epic).** Epic's native AI charting announcement in February 2026 will eventually reach OCHIN Epic users, but OCHIN's deployment timeline adds a layer of complexity. Standalone AI scribe vendors (Abridge, Nabla, Nuance DAX) integrate with Epic but require separate procurement, contracting, and often BAA amendments. These FQHCs face a build-vs-buy-vs-wait decision.

**Tier 3: Unknown infrastructure (92+ FQHCs).** The FQHCs with no identifiable modern EHR in our data face the steepest path to AI adoption. Many of these are smaller organizations (single-site, under 10,000 patients) where the prerequisite for deploying an AI scribe is implementing a modern EHR first — a project that costs $500K-$2M and takes 12-18 months. For these organizations, the AI scribe conversation is premature.

## What NACHC Is Doing (And Why It Matters)

The CHAI-NACHC partnership, established in August 2025, represents the most significant governance framework for AI in the FQHC sector. CHAI (Coalition for Health AI) and NACHC together represent 1,400+ community health centers serving 30 million patients nationally.

The partnership established three things: a vendor certification program for AI tools marketed to FQHCs, an AI curriculum for safety-net organizations, and an equity assessment framework that evaluates whether AI tools perform equitably across patient demographics.

This is important for California FQHC leaders because it creates a defensible standard. When your board asks "how do we evaluate AI vendors?", the CHAI-NACHC framework provides a structured answer that goes beyond vendor demos and ROI projections. It asks: does this tool work for the patient populations we serve? Does it perform accurately for non-English speakers? Does it introduce bias into clinical documentation?

The NACHC AI Action Guide, published separately, provides practical implementation guidance — including evidence that AI-powered readmission prediction models have retained $7.2 million annually at community health centers that deployed them. This is a different category of AI than ambient scribes, but it illustrates the breadth of AI applications that FQHCs should be evaluating beyond documentation.

## What ViVE 2026 Tells Us About Where This Is Going

At the ViVE 2026 conference in March, attendees noted a shift in tone around AI documentation. The phrase repeated most often: "excitement over scribes has died down."

This does not mean ambient AI is failing. It means the market is maturing. When athenahealth gives away AI documentation for free and Epic builds it natively, ambient scribes are no longer a differentiator — they are table stakes. The standalone ambient AI vendors (Abridge, Nabla, Sunoh.ai) will need to demonstrate value beyond documentation to survive as independent companies.

For FQHCs, this maturation is actually good news. It means:

Prices will continue to fall. Vendor lock-in risk is decreasing as EHR-native options proliferate. And the competitive advantage shifts from "we have AI" to "we use AI well" — which favors organizations with strong clinical workflows, thoughtful implementation processes, and engaged clinician champions.

The FQHCs that will benefit most from AI documentation are not the ones that deploy fastest. They are the ones that deploy with clear governance, realistic expectations about PPS-versus-value-based revenue implications, and a workforce that is trained to review and correct AI-generated documentation rather than blindly signing off on it.

## Five Questions Every FQHC Executive Should Ask Before Signing

1. **What is our reimbursement model?** If you are 90%+ PPS, the revenue case for AI scribes is weak. The burnout and retention case may still be strong — but be honest about which problem you are solving.

2. **What does our EHR vendor already include?** Before procuring a third-party AI scribe, check what your EHR is shipping natively. You may be buying a solution to a problem your vendor is about to solve for free.

3. **How will we monitor coding patterns?** If your AI scribe increases HCC capture or wRVU productivity, you need a compliance monitoring process to distinguish appropriate documentation improvement from coding inflation. Build this before deployment, not after an audit.

4. **Does this tool work for our patient population?** Sacramento Native American Health Center's experience with ambient AI surfaced cultural sensitivity challenges in AI-generated documentation. If your FQHC serves predominantly Spanish-speaking, Hmong, Khmer, or indigenous patients, ask the vendor for performance data on those populations — not just English-speaking panels.

5. **What is our governance framework?** The CHAI-NACHC standards provide a starting point. Adapt them to your organization. Assign a clinical leader to own AI quality review. Document your evaluation process. The FQHCs that deploy AI thoughtfully will be the ones that avoid the compliance and quality pitfalls that will inevitably emerge as adoption accelerates.
